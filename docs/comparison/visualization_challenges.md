**Sparsity and small-N**:
With 11 months of data and rolling-origin CV, you have 4 usable folds. F5's WAPE/WBIAS dual-panel and F3's ρ-per-fold chart both put fold index on the x-axis - with 4 points, these charts look nearly empty and a single outlier fold visually dominates. The mitigation is to supplement fold-level points with a confidence band derived from bootstrap resampling _within_ each fold, not just across folds. This gives the line chart visual density without fabricating data. For F1 (attribution shift), sparsity shows up differently: if some channels have very few observed paths in a given bootstrap resample, their credit shares become unstable for mathematical reasons unrelated to the model's quality. You need a minimum-path threshold per channel per resample - paths below that threshold get excluded from the CV computation for that resample, not set to zero. Zero and "not enough data" are different things and conflating them will corrupt F1's error bars.

**Cohort alignment for F6, F7, F8**:
Retention figures require cohorts with enough observed periods to evaluate long-horizon extrapolation. If data spans 11 months, the cohort acquired in month 1 has up to 10 observed rebill periods (for SUB_MONTHLY subscription type). The cohort acquired in month 8 has only 3. This means "late horizon" RMSE in F8 is computed on only 1–3 cohorts, not all cohorts - and you must make this visible in the figure, not average it silently.
One clean approach: plot RMSE by horizon with cohort count annotated on the secondary y-axis or as a fading opacity on the line itself (fewer cohorts = more transparent line). For F9's small multiples, only show cohorts where D90 actuals exist. Don't show a cohort acquired in month 9 and claim D180 extrapolation bias when you have no D180 actuals to compare against.
